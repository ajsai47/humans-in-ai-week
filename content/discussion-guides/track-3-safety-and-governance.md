# Track 3: AI Safety & Governance

## The question

Who decides how AI is built, deployed, and regulated — and how do the rest of us get a say?

## Opening framing (2 minutes — read or adapt)

> Right now, a handful of companies and a handful of governments are making decisions about AI that will affect every person on the planet. The models are being trained. The policies are being written. The norms are being set.
>
> Most people aren't at the table. Not because they don't care — because there's no table to sit at.
>
> Tonight we're creating a table. We're going to talk about who governs AI, what guardrails matter, and what role regular people — not just engineers and politicians — should play in shaping how this technology is used.

## Discussion questions

### 1. The stakes question
"What's the AI risk that keeps you up at night? Not the sci-fi version — the realistic, near-term version."

**Moderator tip**: Let people name their actual fears. Deepfakes, job loss, surveillance, misinformation, bias — all valid. Catalog them on a whiteboard if possible.

### 2. The regulation question
"Should AI be regulated more like pharmaceuticals (strict testing before release), social media (light regulation, fix problems after), or something entirely new?"

**Moderator tip**: This is a framework question. Push people to think in analogies, not abstractions.

### 3. The representation question
"Who should be at the table when AI policy is being made? Right now it's mostly technologists and policymakers. Who's missing?"

**Moderator tip**: This is where the room's diversity becomes an asset. Artists, teachers, healthcare workers, parents — they all have standing here.

### 4. The global question
"AI doesn't respect borders. Can one country's regulation actually work, or does this require global coordination? What does that even look like?"

**Moderator tip**: Especially powerful in international cities or chapters with globally diverse attendees.

### 5. The action question
"If this room could send one recommendation to your national government about AI, what would it be? One sentence."

**Moderator tip**: Go around the room. Write them down. Share them publicly after. This is the "200 cities said X" data point.

## Localization prompt

> "In [country], the current AI regulation landscape is [describe — e.g., 'the EU AI Act is being implemented' / 'there's no federal AI legislation yet' / 'our government just announced an AI strategy']. That means the conversation tonight isn't hypothetical — it's about what's being decided right now, and whether we agree with the direction."

## Audience participation ideas

- **Risk mapping**: Give each table a whiteboard or large paper. Map AI risks on a 2x2 (likelihood vs. severity). Compare across tables
- **One recommendation**: End with each person writing their one-sentence policy recommendation on a card. Collect and publish them
- **Dot voting**: Post 5–6 governance approaches on the wall. Each person gets 3 dots to vote on what they support most
